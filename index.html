Growing Guide
Practical, actionable recommendations for Washington State apple farmers
💧
Irrigation Management
Drip irrigation delivers the best yield efficiency in the dataset — farms using drip systems averaged 2–5% higher lbs/acre than overhead systems in similar conditions.
Target soil moisture between 55–75% (field capacity) throughout fruit development. The dataset shows yields drop sharply when moisture falls below 47%.
Apply 350–550 mm of irrigation water per season depending on rainfall. Columbia Basin farms typically require the highest supplemental irrigation (400–600 mm) due to lower rainfall (~5–10 inches/season).
During fruit sizing (late June–July), maintain consistent soil moisture — irregular wetting causes bitter pit in Honeycrisp and cracking in Fuji.
🌿
Soil & Nutrient Management
Test soil pH annually — apples perform best at pH 6.0–7.0. Sandy loam soils common in Eastern WA drain well but require more frequent fertigation.
Apply nitrogen in split doses: 40% at bloom, 30% at petal fall, 30% during June. Excess nitrogen delays fruit color and reduces storage life.
Honeycrisp is especially calcium-sensitive. Apply calcium foliar sprays every 10–14 days from petal fall through mid-July to prevent bitter pit.
Cover crops between rows improve organic matter over time — especially valuable for organic orchards in the dataset which showed 15–20% variability from conventional farms.
✂️
Pruning & Canopy
Prune during dormancy (January–March) before bud break. Remove approximately 20–25% of canopy annually to maintain light penetration and airflow.
High-density plantings (300+ trees/acre, like F002 Red Delicious at 339 trees/acre) require annual summer pruning to control canopy and improve fruit color.
Central leader systems work well for Gala and Fuji. Spindle systems suit Honeycrisp and Cosmic Crisp in high-density blocks.
Thin fruit to one per cluster (or one per 6–8 inches) by 30 days after full bloom. Proper thinning is the single biggest factor in fruit size and return bloom.
🐛
Pest & Disease Management
Monitor for codling moth using pheromone traps from tight cluster through harvest. Degree-day models help time spray applications precisely — WSU Decision Aid System (DAS) is the standard tool in WA.
Apple scab (Venturia inaequalis) is the top disease threat east of the Cascades during wet springs. Apply protective fungicides during primary scab season (green tip through petal fall).
Fire blight (Erwinia amylovora) is critical for young orchards — the dataset shows several stress year farms with young trees (8–12 yrs) had the most variable yields, partly from disease susceptibility.
Organic operations (flagged in dataset) should incorporate kaolin clay, copper, and biocontrol agents as part of an integrated pest management (IPM) program.
📅
Harvest Planning
Monitor starch-iodine index, firmness (pressure tester), and Brix values together — no single test is reliable alone. WSU Extension guidelines provide target ranges by variety.
Gala and Honeycrisp harvest late July–September. Fuji, Granny Smith, and Cosmic Crisp harvest October–November. Red Delicious spans August–October depending on block elevation.
In stress years (flagged in dataset as temp_stress_days ≥10), check maturity 7–10 days earlier — heat stress accelerates maturity and reduces storage life.
Plan harvest crew scheduling early — labor shortages in Eastern WA during peak season (Sept–Oct) are common. Mechanical harvesting aids are available for high-density blocks.
🏪
Storage & Post-Harvest
Controlled Atmosphere (CA) storage is standard for Fuji, Granny Smith, and Cosmic Crisp — keeps fruit marketable 8–12 months. Honeycrisp benefits from CA but is more susceptible to storage disorders.
Gala and Red Delicious can be regular cold-stored at 30–32°F / 90–95% RH for 3–5 months. Pre-sort before storage — damaged fruit accelerates decay in the entire bin.
Cool fruit to within 5°F of storage temperature within 24 hours of harvest. Delays in pre-cooling reduce shelf life significantly, especially for Honeycrisp.
For direct-to-market small farms: u-pick and farmers market timing aligns with Gala (Aug) and Honeycrisp (Sept) as the highest-value fresh varieties for local sales.

What is Farmgate Price?

How apple prices are determined at the point of sale

Farmgate price is the price a grower receives per pound of apples at the point of first sale — typically to a packing house, co-op, or direct buyer — before any processing, transportation, or retail markup is added.

Think of it this way: If a grocery store sells Honeycrisp for $2.99/lb, the grower might only receive $0.55–0.75/lb. The rest covers packing, cold storage, transportation, and retailer margin.

Typical 2024 farmgate ranges (WA state):

  • Honeycrisp: $0.55 – $0.80/lb
  • Cosmic Crisp: $0.45 – $0.65/lb
  • Gala: $0.26 – $0.38/lb
  • Fuji: $0.26 – $0.34/lb
  • Granny Smith: $0.22 – $0.30/lb
  • Red Delicious: $0.16 – $0.24/lb

Source: USDA Agricultural Marketing Service estimates. Actual prices vary by grade, buyer contract, season, and organic certification.
